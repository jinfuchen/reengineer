\begin{abstract}
Performance is an important aspect of software quality. Performance issues may compromise user experiences, increase the operating cost of the system, and cause field failures. In fact, large software systems failures are often due to performance issues rather than functional bugs. One of the most prevalent performance issues is performance regression.
Due to the lack of real-time performance regression detecting tools, these issues can survive inside the system for a long period, and threaten the quality of the system. Although prior research proposes various automated approaches that predict performance regressions, the detection is conducted after the fact, i.e., after the system is built and deployed in the field or dedicated performance testing environments. We employ a statistically rigorous performance evaluation on 1020 commits from ten releases of \emph{Hadoop} and 135 commits from five releases of \emph{RxJava}, and conduct an empirical study on the JIT prediction of performance regression introducing code changes in these two systems. We find that Performance regressions are not rare instances. Our predictor an average precision of 70.3\% and 54\% percent in the prediction of existence and magnitude of performance regression, respectively. In addition, we find that change measures in dimension Size are more risk-increasing factors. Change measures \emph{Loop} and \emph{Synchronization} are more likely to cause performance regression. 


%In our study, in order to avoid latent performance issues, we inherit theories from bug predicting models and extend them into performance domain, and we propose a just in time performance regression predicting model in commit level. Therefore, with the help of our model, it is applicable for developers to realize performance issues before they commit their changes. 

\end{abstract}
